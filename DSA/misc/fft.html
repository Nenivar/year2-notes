Halving Lemma:

If N>0N>0N>0 is even, the squares of the the NNN complex NthN^{th}Nth roots of unity are the:

N2\frac{N}{2}2N​ complex N2th\frac{N}{2}^{th}2N​th roots of unity

It follows that if we square all the nthn^{th}nth roots of unity, then each n2th\frac{n}{2}^{th}2n​th root of unity is obtained exactly twice;

(ωN0)2,(ωN1)2,…,(ωNN−1)2(\omega^0_N)^2,(\omega^1_N)^2,\dots,(\omega^{N-1}_N)^2 (ωN0​)2,(ωN1​)2,…,(ωNN−1​)2

Inverse Fourier Transform

We will use the Inverse DFT to interpolate polynomials - this relies on a theorem that shows how to invert the DFT:

ai=1n∑j=0n−1yj⋅wn−jia_i = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} y_j \cdot w_n^{-ji} ai​=n1​j=0∑n−1​yj​⋅wn−ji​

This allows us to convert the point-value representation of a polynomial to coefficient form.

  • switches roles of aaa and yyy
  • replaces ωn\omega_nωn​ by ωn−1\omega_n^{-1}ωn−1​
  • divides the final result by nnn ∴\therefore∴ the Inverse DFT can be computed in the same time complexity as the DFT - O(nlog⁡n)O(n\log n)O(nlogn)
