Stable Matching

Situation

S1...SnS_1 ... S_nS1​...Sn​ students applying for summer internships, with E1...EnE_1 ... E_nE1​...En​ employers.

  1. students list employers in order of preference
  2. employers list students in order of preference
  3. employers give offers to students
  4. students accept or decline (and can accept first & decline later)

Unstable matching:

  • there is a pair (S, E) not matched to each other s.t
    • S prefers E to the employer they're matched to
    • E prefers S to the student they're matched to

Algorithm of Happiness

(aka Gale-Shapley)

  1. let iii be the smallest value so that EiE_iEi​ is unmatched
  2. EiE_iEi​ makes an offer to the most desirable student that has not already rejected an offer from EiE_iEi​
  3. the student accepts if:
    • it's the first offer they've received
    • OR they prefer EiE_iEi​ to the employer they're currently matched to
  4. If there are no unmatched employers, stop; else repeat

Correctness

The algorithm terminates after at most n2n^2n2 offers:

  • employers make offers in decreasing order ofpreference
  • so each offer is for a different pair (S, E)
  • there are n2n^2n2 different pairs (S, E)

All students and employers are matched when the algorithm terminates:

  • suppose not everyone is matched - there must be at least one unmatched student sus_usu​ and one unmatched employer eue_ueu​
  • eue_ueu​ must have made an offer to all the students, including sus_usu​
  • sus_usu​ received at least one offer - after accepting her first offer she will have remained matched for the rest of the running of the algorithm.
  • so sus_usu​ is in fact matched - contradiction

The matching found by the algorithm is stable:

  • employers make offers in descending order of preference
  • students only trade up
  • suppose the matching is unstable - i.e. s1s_1s1​ is matched to e1e_1e1​ but prefers e2e_2e2​; e2e_2e2​ is matched to s2s_2s2​ but prefers s1s_1s1​
  • e2e_2e2​ must have made an offer to s1s_1s1​ before making an offer to s2s_2s2​ - s1s_1s1​ rejected the offer, so must prefer e1e_1e1​ to e2e_2e2​ - contradiction

Implementation tip

Inverse each student's list of employers ordered by preference

→\rightarrow→ you can answer quickly when the number of employers is large;

'Does SkS_kSk​ prefer employer iii to employer jjj?

Check whether inverse-pref[k, i] < inverse_pref[k, j]

Perfection

Stable matchings are not unique.

Lemma:

If SSS rejects an offer from EEE, then (S,E)(S, E)(S,E) is not feasible

Theorem:

The algorithm pairs each employer EEE with best(E), EEE's favourite student SSS for which (S,E)(S, E)(S,E) is feasible

Corollary:

The same matching is reached regardless of the ordering of the employers

Consequently, the algorithm assigns each student SSS to worst(S), SSS's least favourite employer EEE of those for which there is a stable matching containing (S,E)(S, E)(S,E)
